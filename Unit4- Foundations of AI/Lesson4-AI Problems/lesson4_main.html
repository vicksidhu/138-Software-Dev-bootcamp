Problems and Challenges With AI

Section 1 — Why AI Isn't Perfect

1. Bias — The AI Learned Our Prejudices

Remember how we learned that AI learns from enormous amounts of text written by humans? Books, websites, articles, social media posts... billions of words. The problem: humans have biases, and the AI absorbed them all.

📚 Simple Analogy — Learning from a Biased Textbook

Imagine a child who learns about professions only by reading books from the 1950s. Every doctor in those books is male. Every nurse is female. After reading thousands of such books, the child now has biased assumptions because that's all they were exposed to.

An AI trained on historical text has absorbed the same kind of historical biases. It didn't choose them. It just learned from what it read.

Bias shows up in many forms:

👩‍⚕️
Gender bias

Assuming a doctor is male, a nurse is female, or that certain jobs belong to certain genders

📰
Representation bias

Certain viewpoints, languages, and communities are underrepresented in the training data

🔁
Confirmation bias

Popular opinions online are more represented, making the AI lean toward majority views

Bias also has a connection to privacy and intellectual property. The text used to train AI was often taken from the internet without permission. This means:

  • Private information that was online may have been swept into training data
  • Books, articles, and creative works were used to train AI without the author's consent or payment
  • The AI might reproduce pieces of copyrighted text without realising it
⚡ Important to Know

None of this means AI is evil or should be abandoned. It means we need to be aware of these limitations and not blindly trust AI outputs on sensitive topics. Always apply human judgement.

2. Hallucinations — When AI Confidently Makes Things Up

This is perhaps the most important AI problem to understand. A hallucination is when an AI states something that is completely made up but presents it with total confidence, as if it were a well-established fact.

👻 What a Hallucination Looks Like

You ask an AI: "Who wrote the 1987 book 'The Architecture of Learning'?"

The AI confidently replies: "That book was written by Professor James T. Hawkins of Stanford University and won the National Book Award that year."

The problem? That book doesn't exist. Professor Hawkins doesn't exist. The award never happened. The AI invented every single detail but said it with complete confidence, no hesitation, no "I'm not sure."

Why does this happen? Remember that an AI's job is to predict the most likely next word. When it doesn't know the answer, it doesn't say "I don't know" by default, it generates a plausible-sounding answer based on patterns. It's doing what it always does (predicting text), but in a situation where it has no real knowledge to draw from.

🎯 Strict Definition — What IS and ISN'T a Hallucination

A hallucination is specifically when AI generates a factually wrong statement that sounds confident and real. It's a confident lie the AI doesn't know it's telling.

Not all AI errors are hallucinations. If the AI says "I don't know," that's not a hallucination that's honesty. If the AI misunderstands your question and answers a different question correctly, that's a misunderstanding, not a hallucination. If it makes a maths error, that's a calculation mistake, not a hallucination. Hallucination specifically means: generated false information presented as true.

📚
Fake citations

Making up journal articles, book titles, and author names that don't exist

👤
Fake people

Inventing biographies for people who don't exist, or mixing up real people's facts

📅
Wrong facts

Getting statistics, dates, laws, or scientific results completely wrong but sounding sure

💻
Broken code

Generating code that uses libraries or functions that don't exist

3. Safety — AI Can Be Misused

AI is a powerful tool. Like all powerful tools, it can be used for harm if the wrong people use it in the wrong ways.

💬
Unsafe outputs

AI generating harmful, offensive, dangerous, or illegal content if not properly restricted

🔪
Malicious use

Bad actors using AI to generate disinformation, scam messages, or dangerous instructions at scale

🤖
Prompt injection

Hiding instructions inside content (like a document) that trick the AI into doing unintended things

4. The Black Box — Nobody Knows Why AI Says What It Says

This one surprises many people. Even the engineers who built ChatGPT cannot fully explain why it gives a particular answer. The AI is what's called a "black box" — you can see what goes in (your prompt) and what comes out (the answer), but the process in the middle is almost impossible to interpret.

📦 The Black Box Analogy

Imagine a machine that takes vegetables in one end and produces meals at the other end. You can see the vegetables going in and the meal coming out. But the machine has no windows, no labels, no on/off switches you can examine — you have no idea what's happening inside. You can experiment (put in carrots, see what meal comes out), but you can't read the recipe.

Neural networks with billions of parameters are like this. Each output is the result of millions of tiny mathematical operations happening simultaneously. There's no single "decision point" to point to.

🚨 A Common Misconception

People often think: "Can't you just ask the AI to explain its reasoning?" This seems logical — but it doesn't actually work. When you ask an AI why it said something, it generates a plausible-sounding explanation — but this explanation is itself just another AI output. It's not a window into the actual calculations. The AI is making up a reason that sounds reasonable, not reading out its actual decision process.

Think of it like asking a calculator "why did you get 42?" — the calculator can't answer that. It just computed. The AI is the same, except it's very good at generating convincing-sounding answers.

Controlling AI — Inference Parameters

Section 2 — The Dials That Change AI's Behaviour

Before we look at how to improve AI for specific jobs, let's understand the basic controls available. When you use an AI, there are several parameters (settings) you can adjust to change how it responds. These are the simplest way to shape AI behaviour.

🎛 Analogy — Adjusting a Music Mixer

A DJ at a concert has a mixing board with many dials — bass, treble, volume, reverb. The underlying music is the same, but by adjusting the dials, they can completely change how it sounds. AI inference parameters are those dials. The model is the same underneath, but the output changes dramatically based on how the dials are set.

The Four Main Parameters

Run this file to see all four parameters and how changing them affects AI output in real time.

🌡
Temperature

What it controls: How "creative" or "random" the AI is.

Low (0.1): Safe, predictable, repetitive. Always gives the most likely answer.

High (1.5): Wild, creative, surprising — sometimes too surprising (nonsense).

Sweet spot: Around 0.7 for most tasks.

🔝
Top P

What it controls: The variety of words the AI considers when picking the next word.

Low (0.1): Only considers the very top, most likely words (safe but boring).

High (0.9): Considers a much wider range of possible words (more variety).

Works together with Temperature.

🔢
Top K

What it controls: A hard limit on how many word options the AI considers.

Top K = 5: Only ever considers the 5 most likely next words.

Top K = 50: Considers up to 50 possible next words, giving more variety.

Think of it as the size of the AI's "menu."

📏
Max Tokens

What it controls: The maximum length of the AI's response.

Low (50): Forces very short responses.

High (2000): Allows long, detailed responses.

One token ≈ ¾ of a word. 100 tokens ≈ 75 words.

Limitations of a Basic AI Model

Even with perfect parameter settings, a base AI model has two big limitations out of the box:

📅
Knowledge cutoff

AI training stops at a certain date. It knows nothing about events after that date. Ask it about last week's news and it simply won't know — it's not connected to the internet by default.

🔒
No private data

A base model has never seen your company's documents, your customer data, your internal policies, or anything that wasn't publicly available on the internet. It can't answer questions about your specific business.

💡 Why Not Just Train a New AI?

The obvious solution might seem to be: "Let's just train our own AI from scratch with our private data." This is not realistic for almost anyone. Training a large language model from scratch requires thousands of high-end GPUs running for months, and costs between $10 million and $100 million+. Even large tech companies rarely do this — everyone uses existing foundation models as the starting point.

So how do we give an AI access to our private data without retraining from scratch? That's what Sections 4 and 5 are about — RAG and fine-tuning.

Prompting — Asking AI the Right Way

Section 3 — The First and Simplest Fix

Two Different Things: Prompting vs Prompt Engineering

These two terms sound similar but mean different things:

TermWho Does ItWhat It IsGoal
Prompting End users (you, me, everyone) The skill of writing good questions/instructions to get useful results Get a good answer for your specific task right now
Prompt Engineering Developers building AI products A structured, scientific process of testing and optimising prompts at scale Build a reliable system that works well for thousands of users
🍳 A Kitchen Analogy

Prompting is like knowing how to cook for yourself — knowing that you need to season the water, not overcook the pasta, and how to balance flavours. Anyone can learn this. It improves your results immediately.

Prompt engineering is like being a head chef at a restaurant — testing recipes hundreds of times, writing precise instructions for other chefs, and making sure every dish that leaves the kitchen is consistent and perfect.

Why Does Prompting Matter?

Prompting is the cheapest, fastest, and simplest way to improve what you get from AI. Before spending money on more complex solutions (like RAG or fine-tuning), a well-written prompt can get you surprisingly far.

❌ Bad Prompt

✅ Much Better Prompt

You are a science communicator writing for a general audience with no science background.

Use simple language, avoid jargon, and include one concrete real-world example in each paragraph.

Format: three paragraphs with no headings.

The Core Prompting Principles

🎭
Give it a role

"You are a helpful customer service agent for a UK bank" tells the AI exactly how to behave, what tone to use, and what to know.

🎯
Be specific

"Summarise this" is vague. "Summarise this in 3 bullet points for a non-technical manager" is specific. Specific = better results.

📋
Specify the format

Tell it exactly how you want the output: "as a numbered list", "in a table", "in 2 paragraphs", "under 100 words". Don't leave format to chance.

📚
Give examples

Show it what a good answer looks like. "Here's an example of the format I want: [example]" is one of the most powerful techniques.

🧠
Ask it to think

"Think step by step before answering" or "explain your reasoning first" dramatically improves accuracy on complex tasks.

🚫
Say what NOT to do

"Do not use bullet points. Do not mention competitors. Do not use technical jargon." Negative instructions are just as important as positive ones.

RAG — Giving AI Your Own Information

Section 4 — Retrieval-Augmented Generation

The Problem RAG Solves

We established that a base AI model doesn't know about your private data. If you work at a hospital and want an AI to answer questions about your hospital's specific policies, procedures, and patient history guidelines — a standard AI can't do that. It never saw those documents.

You could paste the documents into the prompt every time — but what if you have thousands of documents? You can't paste all of them. This is where RAG comes in.

📚 The Library Assistant Analogy

Imagine you hired a very smart research assistant who has read millions of general books (that's the base AI model). But they've never been inside your company's private library.

RAG is like this: when you ask your assistant a question, before they answer, they run into your private library, grab the most relevant pages, read them, and then answer your question using both their general knowledge and those specific pages.

They don't read the entire library every time. They just retrieve the relevant parts for this specific question.

RAG sits in the middle of three approaches — compare them below:

Prompting only RAG ← sweet spot Fine-tuning
Cheap & Simple Expensive & Complex
ApproachCostComplexityBest for
Prompting only✅ Free✅ SimpleGeneral tasks, no private data needed

✅ Key Takeaway

RAG is popular in real businesses because it solves the most common business problem — "our AI needs to know about our specific documents" — without the enormous cost and complexity of fine-tuning. It's relatively quick to set up, the knowledge base is easy to update (just add new documents), and it works very well. We'll explore it in much more depth later in the course.

Fine-tuning — Teaching AI New Habits

Section 5 — The Most Powerful (and Expensive) Approach

What Is Fine-tuning?

Fine-tuning is continuing to train an existing AI model using your own private data. The base model already knows how to do language. Fine-tuning adjusts it to behave differently — to use your company's tone of voice, know your specific domain deeply, or follow a very specific format every time.

🎓 The New Employee Analogy

Imagine hiring a brilliant, well-educated new employee (base model). They already know how to read, write, communicate, and think. Now you send them to a 3-month training programme specifically for your company — they learn your company's tone of voice, your industry's specific terminology, your internal processes, and how to write in exactly your brand style.

After training, they don't just know your style — they embody it. Every response naturally sounds like your company without being told to. That transformation is what fine-tuning does.

What Fine-tuning Actually Changes

Fine-tuning changes the actual weights inside the model — the millions of numbers that determine how it behaves. This is the key difference from prompting and RAG:

✍️
Prompting

You tell the model what to do every single time you use it. It forgets between sessions. Like giving someone a script to read.

📚
RAG

You give the model relevant documents to read before answering. It uses that info but is still the same model underneath.

🎓
Fine-tuning

You permanently change how the model works. The new behaviour is baked in. No script needed every time — it just behaves that way naturally.

⚠️ The Common Misconception

Many businesses think: "We'll fine-tune our AI on our internal documents and it'll know all our company knowledge." This often disappoints people. Fine-tuning is better at teaching how to write than what to know. For specific facts and knowledge, RAG is usually the better choice.

Use fine-tuning for: consistent brand voice, specific response formats, industry-specific writing style.
Use RAG for: answering questions from specific documents, accessing up-to-date information.

When Is Fine-tuning the Right Choice?

✅
Good fit

You need a very specific tone or writing style consistently. You have high volume usage where big system prompts are expensive. You need the model to reliably output a specific format.

❌
Not a good fit

You mainly need the AI to know about specific documents or facts. Your knowledge base changes frequently. You're on a tight budget. You want to understand why the AI says what it says.

✅ Key Takeaway

Fine-tuning is real training — it permanently changes the model. It's the most powerful tool but also the most expensive and complex. Most businesses solve their AI problems with prompting + RAG before ever considering fine-tuning. Think of it as a last resort, not a first step.
